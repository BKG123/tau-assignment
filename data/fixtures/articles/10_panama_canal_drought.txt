## Supply Chain Disruptions that Defined 2024: A Resilience Perspective

Supply chain disruptions in 2024 have been significantly influenced by a series of high-impact events, including the Red Sea crisis, the Baltimore bridge collapse, the Panama Canal drought, and labor strikes at East and Gulf Coast ports. These incidents have created ripple effects across the U.S. economy, straining logistics networks and causing delays in the movement of goods. Each event highlights vulnerabilities in supply chain infrastructure and the need for more robust contingency planning.

### **The 2024 Hurricane Season**

The 2024 hurricane season, particularly Hurricanes Helene and Milton, left a profound impact on the warehousing and logistics industries. These events disrupted operations and strained businesses across the supply chain.

Hurricane Helene caused [widespread damage](https://www.steptoe.com/en/news-publications/stepwise-risk-outlook/stepwise-risk-outlook-deep-dive-hurricanes-typhoons-leading-to-escalating-supply-chain-risks.html) to transportation infrastructure in the Southeast, with flooding washing out roads, highways, and rail lines. Some of these critical routes are expected to take up to a year to repair. Both Helene and Milton also caused significant power outages and port closures, further delaying transportation and compounding the challenges faced by businesses.

Manufacturing and warehousing were especially affected. Hurricane Helene forced several automotive and healthcare manufacturers to shut down operations for weeks, causing shortages of vital goods. Automotive plants nationwide were disrupted by delays in critical components, while [healthcare shortages](https://www.cidrap.umn.edu/resilient-drug-supply/iv-fluid-shortages-worsened-hurricane-helene-likely-linger-despite-larger) stemming from a damaged manufacturing facility lingered for months. Reconstruction and recovery efforts are still underway, underscoring the long-term consequences of natural disasters on supply chain operations.

### **The Red Sea Crisis: Navigating a Global Bottleneck**

The Red Sea crisis has emerged as a critical chokepoint for global trade, with escalating geopolitical tensions in the region disrupting key shipping lanes. This has led to delays in the delivery of goods originating from Asia, Europe, and the Middle East. Companies have been forced to reroute shipments through alternative, often more expensive, pathways, exacerbating supply chain inefficiencies.

Additionally, the crisis has caused ripple effects across other transportation networks as companies seek alternative shipping methods. Ports along the East Coast of the United States, already grappling with labor strikes and congestion, have faced additional pressure from rerouted cargo.

To adapt, many manufacturers and retailers have been forced to reevaluate their supply chain strategies, including diversifying their sourcing locations and increasing safety stock levels to buffer against future disruptions. However, these measures come with added costs and logistical challenges, particularly for smaller businesses with limited resources.

The crisis has also accelerated the adoption of supply chain visibility tools, enabling retailers to track shipments better and anticipate delays. While these adaptations may improve resilience in the long term, the immediate impact of the Red Sea crisis has exposed significant vulnerabilities in supply chains, highlighting the need for greater flexibility and contingency planning.

### **Baltimore Bridge Collapse: Aging Infrastructure’s Toll**

In March, The Dali, a 948 ft cargo ship hit Baltimore’s Francis Scott Key Bridge, sending the 1.5-mile bridge into the Patapsco River and killing six members of a road crew working on the bridge.

The collapse of the bridge severely impacted regional transportation networks. This critical infrastructure failure severed a key transportation artery.

The closure disrupted freight movement along the East Coast, causing significant delays in trucking and rail operations and forcing logistics providers to use longer, less efficient routes, increasing fuel costs and delivery times. Local industries, particularly those in manufacturing and retail struggled to maintain inventory levels, underscoring the fragility of aging infrastructure in the U.S.

The collapse also had implications for the Port of Baltimore. The bridge served as a vital connection for transporting goods from the port to inland destinations, and its failure created significant logistical challenges. Trucks transporting cargo from the port were forced to take lengthy detours, increasing transit times and costs.

The disruption also impacted the port’s ability to handle incoming shipments efficiently. With reduced accessibility, some shipping companies diverted their cargo to other ports, leading to a temporary decline in throughput at the Port of Baltimore.

The collapse of the Baltimore Bridge has also highlighted broader concerns about the state of U.S. infrastructure and its impact on supply chain resilience. Aging infrastructure across the country poses a significant risk to the stability of logistics networks, and the bridge failure has underscored the urgency of investing in maintenance and modernization.

### **Labor Strikes at East and Gulf Coast Ports: Ensuring Continuity**

The International Longshoremen’s Association (ILA) and the United States Maritime Alliance (USMX) have held on-again/off-again contract discussions over the past year. The contract, which expired on September 30, led to a three-day strike at East and Gulf Coast ports in October. The two sides agreed upon an extension through January 15 to reach an agreement.

However, the uncertainty surrounding labor negotiations has led to a surge in preemptive shipping activity as businesses rush to secure inventory ahead of a possible work stoppage. This has created congestion at some East and Gulf ports and warehouses, amplifying delays and costs.

### **Panama Canal Drought: Adapting to Environmental Challenges**

The ongoing drought in the Panama Canal region also compounded supply chain challenges. With water levels at historic lows in 2023 and 2024, the canal’s capacity to accommodate large vessels was significantly reduced. As a result, shipping companies faced restrictions on vessel sizes and cargo loads, leading to backlogs and increased shipping costs. This, in particular, affected U.S. imports of goods from Asia, as the canal is a critical transit point for these shipments.

In response to these challenges, businesses and logistics providers have been exploring long-term solutions to mitigate the risks associated with the Panama Canal’s water shortages. These include diversifying shipping routes, investing in technologies to optimize cargo loads, and increasing the use of smaller vessels better suited for the canal’s current constraints.
